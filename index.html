Why Reservation?
The Representation Gap

Women constitute roughly half of India's population, but have never held more than 15% of Lok Sabha seats. The gap between democratic promise and political reality is what the Women's Reservation Act seeks to address.

02
A 77-Year Journey
The Timeline

From the Constituent Assembly's first debates to the current 2026 delimitation bills — nine turning points that define India's struggle to guarantee women's political representation.

1946
Origin
The Constituent Assembly: One Woman in the Room

When the Constituent Assembly convened to write India's Constitution, Sarojini Naidu was the only woman present in the founding session. The question of reserving seats for women in Parliament was debated — and rejected — by the framers.

What Happened

The Constituent Assembly debated whether women should have reserved seats in Parliament, similar to the reservations being created for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. The proposal was ultimately rejected. The framers chose to give women equal voting rights and the right to contest from any constituency — but no guaranteed seats.

The Reasoning
  • Women had equal citizenship and voting rights — formal equality was deemed sufficient
  • Concerns that reserved seats might be seen as diminishing women's capability
  • SC/ST reservation was justified by caste-based exclusion; gender was treated differently
  • Many women members themselves opposed separate reservation

The irony that one of India's greatest women leaders — Sarojini Naidu — was the sole woman in the founding session became a symbol of the contradiction at the heart of Indian democracy: a Constitution that promised equality but produced profoundly unequal representation.

1992–93
Enacted
The 73rd & 74th Amendments: The Precedent Is Set

Parliament passed the Constitution (73rd Amendment) Act, 1992 and the Constitution (74th Amendment) Act, 1992 — both coming into force in 1993 (24 April and 1 June respectively). These amendments mandated one-third reservation for women in panchayats and urban local bodies, creating the world's largest experiment in democratic gender representation, and establishing the template that would later be applied to Parliament itself.

What Was Enacted
  • Constitution (73rd Amendment) Act, 1992 (in force 24 April 1993) — Article 243D(3): not less than 1/3 of panchayat seats reserved for women
  • Constitution (74th Amendment) Act, 1992 (in force 1 June 1993) — Article 243T: not less than 1/3 of municipality seats reserved for women
  • Within SC/ST reserved seats, 1/3 further reserved for women of those communities
  • Reserved seats rotate among constituencies in each panchayat
What It Proved
  • By 2009, over 1 million women held elected office in local bodies — more than any other country
  • Women's actual representation reached 42.3% — beyond the mandated 33%
  • Early fears of "proxy" women (nagging wives ruling in name only) proved largely unfounded
  • Studies showed women prioritised water, sanitation, schools — changing local policy outcomes
  • The Rajiv Gandhi-era vision became the strongest argument for extending reservation upward

The Standing Committee's 2009 report credited "the vision of former Prime Minister Shri Rajiv Gandhi" for the panchayat reservation concept — though the Acts themselves were introduced and passed under P.V. Narasimha Rao's government in 1992. Whatever its political parentage, the panchayat experiment became the single strongest empirical argument for extending reservation upward to Parliament.

1996
Lapsed
The 81st Amendment Bill: First Attempt, First Failure

The Constitution (81st Amendment) Bill was introduced in the 11th Lok Sabha — the first formal attempt to reserve parliamentary seats for women. It was referred to a Joint Committee chaired by Geeta Mukherjee, whose 1996 report remains a foundational document. The bill lapsed when the Lok Sabha was dissolved.

The Geeta Mukherjee Committee Recommendations (1996)
  • One-third reservation of parliamentary seats for women — supported
  • Reservation should last 15 years, then be reviewed
  • OBC women should be considered for sub-reservation "at appropriate time"
  • Reservation should be considered for Rajya Sabha and Legislative Councils
  • Committee agreed the bill was necessary — but lapsed before a vote
Why It Failed
  • The 11th Lok Sabha was dissolved before the bill could be voted on
  • Fundamentally a timing failure, not a rejection
  • OBC sub-quota demand from parties like SP and RJD was beginning to emerge as a flashpoint
  • No cross-party whip or coalition agreement existed
1998–99
Lapsed × 2
The 84th & 85th Amendment Bills: Parliament in Uproar

Two more bills were introduced in successive Lok Sabhas — and both lapsed on dissolution. These debates were marked by dramatic scenes: MPs from regional parties tore up copies of the bill on the floor of the House, physically disrupted proceedings, and demanded an OBC sub-quota within the women's quota as a condition of support.

The OBC Flashpoint

Parties led by Mulayam Singh Yadav (SP) and Laloo Prasad Yadav (RJD) argued that without a sub-quota for Other Backward Class women, the bill would primarily benefit upper-caste women. This "quota within quota" demand became the central political deadlock — one that would persist through 2023.

The Gill Formula Alternative

The Election Commission proposed requiring political parties to nominate a minimum percentage of women candidates (the "Gill Formula"). This was widely rejected — critics argued parties would nominate women only in unwinnable seats, making the provision cosmetic.

Former Uttar Pradesh Chief Minister Mulayam Singh Yadav suggested in 2010 that the bill should not be enacted, citing concerns that it would encourage male parliamentarians to engage in inappropriate behaviour toward female colleagues — a statement widely condemned.

2008–14
Passed Rajya Sabha, Lapsed in Lok Sabha
The 108th Amendment: Closest Miss

The Constitution (108th Amendment) Bill was introduced in the Rajya Sabha in May 2008 under the UPA government. After a two-year Standing Committee examination, it was passed by the Rajya Sabha in March 2010 — the first time either House passed such a bill. But the Lok Sabha never voted on it, and it lapsed in 2014 when the 15th Lok Sabha was dissolved.

Key Provisions of the 108th Amendment Bill
  • 1/3 reservation in Lok Sabha and all state legislative assemblies
  • Quota-within-quota for SC/ST women within the reserved seats
  • Reservation for 15 years, then reviewable
  • Reserved seats to rotate after each general election
  • No OBC sub-quota (this was the core political controversy)
The 36th Standing Committee Report (2009)
  • Chaired by Jayanthi Natarajan (INC)
  • Conducted 14 sittings; visited 10 states; heard NGOs, parties, state governments
  • Unanimous: reservation is "a valid and necessary strategy"
  • Rejected double-member constituencies — would make women "second class MPs"
  • Opposed leaving nomination to parties — would produce only "losing seats"
  • Minority note from SP/RJD members: demanded OBC sub-quota

The Committee concluded: "The Women's Reservation Bill should be passed in Parliament and put in action without further delay." More than 12 years had elapsed since the Geeta Mukherjee Committee's report — and another 13 would pass before that finally happened.

— 36th Report, Standing Committee on Personnel, Public Grievances, Law and Justice (December 2009)

Sep 2023
Passed Both Houses
The Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam: Parliament Finally Votes

On September 19, 2023 — the first day of business in the new Parliament building — the Modi government introduced the Constitution (128th Amendment) Bill. It passed Lok Sabha on September 20 with 454 votes in favour and 2 against, and Rajya Sabha on September 21 with 214 votes in favour and none against. President Droupadi Murmu signed it into law on September 28, 2023, making it the Constitution's 106th Amendment. But a critical condition was embedded: the reservation would only activate after a new census and delimitation exercise.

What the Act Does
  • Inserts Article 330A — reserves seats for women in Lok Sabha
  • Inserts Article 332A — reserves seats in every State Assembly
  • Inserts Article 239AA amendment — covers Delhi assembly
  • Inserts Article 334A — the commencement condition
  • One-third of SC/ST seats also reserved for women of those communities
  • 15-year sunset clause, extendable by Parliament
  • Seats rotate after each delimitation exercise
The Commencement Condition (Article 334A)

The reservation provisions "shall come into effect after an exercise of delimitation is undertaken for this purpose after the relevant figures for the first census taken after commencement of the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 have been published."

In plain language: the reservation cannot begin until India completes a Census and then a Delimitation exercise based on that Census data.

Political Context
  • Introduced in the first session of the new Parliament building — symbolic timing
  • Near-unanimous support: BJP, INC, and most regional parties voted in favour
  • OBC sub-quota demand was not included — same sticking point as 2008
  • Opposition questioned sincerity: census condition meant no reservation before 2029 at earliest
  • Home Minister Amit Shah assured: census and delimitation would proceed after 2024 elections
What It Does NOT Do
  • No reservation in Rajya Sabha or state Legislative Councils
  • No OBC sub-quota within women's quota
  • Does not specify which constituencies will be reserved — left to delimitation
  • Does not fix a commencement date — contingent on census and delimitation
  • Does not apply to currently sitting Houses until dissolution

28 Sep 2023
Enacted
Presidential Assent & Gazette Notification: The 106th Amendment

President Droupadi Murmu signed the bill and the Official Gazette notification was published the same day — formally making women's reservation a constitutional right. The bill became the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023. Implementation remained contingent on census and delimitation.

The Constitutional Articles Inserted
  • Article 330A — Reservation of seats for women in the House of the People (Lok Sabha)
  • Article 332A — Reservation of seats for women in the Legislative Assembly of every State
  • Article 239AA (amended) — Clauses (ba), (bb), (bc) added for Delhi Legislative Assembly reservation
  • Article 334A — Governs when reservation takes effect: after delimitation following publication of Census figures; 15-year sunset; rotation after each delimitation

The gazette notification also clarified that the amendments shall not affect the composition of any existing House until that House is dissolved — meaning the 17th Lok Sabha (2019–2024) continued without any reserved seats.

2025–26
Unfolding
The Census Is Announced — But the Deadline Shifts

The 2021 Census was delayed by COVID. In June 2025, the government notified Census 2027 with a reference date of March 1, 2027. This meant delimitation based on Census 2027 data could not realistically be completed before the 2029 Lok Sabha elections — making it virtually certain the reservation would not apply in 2029 either.

The Timeline Problem
  • Census 2021 postponed due to COVID-19 pandemic
  • Census 2027 notified in Gazette on June 4, 2025 (reference date: March 1, 2027)
  • The 2023 Act requires delimitation based on "the first census after commencement"
  • Last Delimitation Commission (2002) took 6 years to complete its work
  • 2029 Lok Sabha elections would arrive before any 2027 Census-based delimitation could finish
  • Result: reservation effectively deferred to 2034 elections at earliest
Political Pressure
  • Critics argued the Census-delimitation conditionality in Article 334A meant the 2023 Act had no guaranteed implementation timeline
  • PM Modi published op-ed on April 9, 2026 urging urgency on women's representation
  • Pressure mounted to delink reservation from Census 2027 and use older data
  • Three new bills were introduced in Parliament on April 16, 2026
Apr 2026
Defeated in Lok Sabha
The 131st Amendment Bills: Introduced, Debated, Defeated

On April 16, 2026, three bills were introduced in a special parliamentary session to expand Lok Sabha to 850 seats, base delimitation on the 2011 Census, and activate women's reservation for 2029. On April 17, the 131st Amendment Bill was put to a vote — and defeated. With 298 votes in favour and 230 against (out of 528 members), the government fell short of the 352 required for a two-thirds majority. The Delimitation Bill was then withdrawn. This was the first time since 2014 that a constitutional amendment bill failed to pass in the Lok Sabha. Women's reservation under the 2023 Act remains on the books — but still cannot activate without a delimitation exercise.

The Three Bills (April 16–17, 2026)
  • Constitution (131st Amendment) Bill — Would have expanded Lok Sabha maximum from 550 to 850 seats (815 from states + 35 from UTs); reverted to population-proportional delimitation; gave Parliament authority to choose which census is used; amended Article 334A to delink women's reservation from Census 2027
  • Delimitation Bill, 2026 — Would have constituted a Delimitation Commission to use 2011 Census data (the latest published census)
  • Union Territories Laws (Amendment) Bill, 2026 — Applied parallel changes to Puducherry, Delhi, and J&K assemblies
Why It Failed — The Vote
  • Constitutional amendment requires 2/3 of members present and voting (Article 368)
  • 528 members present — needed 352 votes to pass
  • Result: 298 in favour, 230 against — fell short by 54 votes
  • Opposition (INDIA alliance) voted against as a bloc
  • Southern state parties (DMK, Congress, TMC) opposed: feared population-based reallocation would reduce their seats in a larger House
  • Government withdrew the Delimitation Bill after the constitutional amendment fell
  • First Modi government constitutional amendment to fail in Lok Sabha
Opposition Arguments
  • Population-based delimitation would punish southern states for successful family planning — Tamil Nadu would drop from 39 to ~32 seats, Kerala from 20 to ~15
  • Women's reservation should be delinked from delimitation entirely, not made contingent on a contested seat-expansion exercise
  • 2023 Act was passed unanimously — the new bills added controversial elements that had nothing to do with women's reservation
  • Both sides disputed the characterisation of the vote — the government argued opposition blocked women's reservation; opposition argued they had voted unanimously for it in 2023 and were opposing the delimitation package, not the reservation itself
Where Things Stand After Defeat
  • The 106th Amendment (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam) was officially notified as in force from April 16, 2026
  • However, Article 334A still ties reservation's activation to delimitation after a census — that trigger has not changed
  • With the 2026 bills defeated, women's reservation cannot realistically apply to the 2029 Lok Sabha elections
  • Earliest likely implementation: 2034, pending Census 2027 completion and a fresh delimitation process
  • Government may bring revised bills in a future session

03
The 106th Amendment Act
What the Law Says

The Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 — formally the Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam — inserts four new constitutional provisions. Here is what each does, in plain language.

🏛️
Lok Sabha Reservation

One-third of all Lok Sabha seats shall be reserved for women. Within the seats already reserved for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, one-third must go to women of those communities.

Article 330A
🏢
State Assembly Reservation

One-third of all seats in every State Legislative Assembly shall be reserved for women — with the same SC/ST sub-quota. This applies to all 28 states and the NCT of Delhi.

Article 332A + 239AA
⏳
Commencement Condition

The reservation only takes effect after: (1) the first census conducted after the Act's commencement is published; AND (2) a delimitation exercise is completed based on that census. This is the critical conditionality now being renegotiated.

Article 334A(1)
📅
15-Year Sunset Clause

The reservation will automatically lapse 15 years from the date of commencement — but Parliament may extend it by passing a law. This mirrors the SC/ST reservation sunset model, though SC/ST reservation has been extended repeatedly since 1950.

Article 334A(1)
🔄
Rotation of Seats

The specific constituencies designated as "women-reserved" will rotate after each delimitation exercise. Parliament will pass a law determining how rotation works. This prevents any constituency from being permanently reserved.

Article 334A(3)
🛡️
No Retrospective Effect

The amendment does not disturb any existing House of the People, State Assembly, or Delhi Assembly until that House is dissolved through the normal electoral cycle. The 17th Lok Sabha continued unchanged until its dissolution in 2024.

04
Contested Territory
The Core Debates

These are not simple questions. The debates around women's reservation have engaged constitutional lawyers, political theorists, women's organisations, and parties across the spectrum for three decades. Here are the strongest arguments on both sides, drawn from the parliamentary record.

Arguments For Reservation
✅Women constitute 50% of the population but less than 15% of Parliament. Without structural intervention, informal barriers — patriarchal party structures, male incumbency advantage, lack of campaign financing — will perpetuate this gap indefinitely.
✅The panchayat experience (post-1993) proves it works: women proved capable legislators; "proxy" fears were unfounded; actual representation exceeded the mandated quota; women prioritised different policy issues than male legislators.
✅Article 15(3) of the Constitution explicitly permits the state to make special provisions for women. Reservation is constitutionally valid, not a deviation from equality — it corrects systemic disadvantage.
✅International evidence: countries with legislative gender quotas achieve higher women's representation faster. India ranked among the lowest major democracies — below Bangladesh and Brazil — as of 2023.
✅Leaving nomination to political parties (the Gill Formula) fails — parties give women unwinnable seats. Constitutional mandate is the only reliable route.
Arguments Against / Critiques
⚠️Without OBC sub-quotas, the bill will primarily benefit upper-caste and urban women. The argument: gender alone is an insufficient category; class and caste must be addressed within the quota for genuine representation of all women. (Core demand of SP, RJD, and allied parties)
⚠️Rotation of reserved constituencies prevents legislators from building long-term constituency relationships. A woman elected from a reserved seat may find the seat unreserved next election — undermining accountability.
⚠️Seat reservation restricts voter choice in reserved constituencies — voters cannot elect a man even if they prefer to. Some argue party-level quotas or dual-member constituencies are less coercive alternatives.
⚠️Symbolic passage with delayed implementation. The 2023 Act's Census-delimitation conditionality effectively deferred implementation to 2034+. Critics called it an "election jumla" — a promise with no delivery mechanism.
⚠️Numerical presence ≠ substantive representation. Research shows women sarpanches (village heads) from marginalised communities often face barriers to actual decision-making despite holding office. Entry is necessary but not sufficient.
05
April 2026 · Outcome
Two Debates, One Bill: Understanding What Actually Failed
⚠ Critical Distinction — Read This First
Debate A: Women's Reservation ✓ Settled

Should Parliament reserve one-third of seats for women? This was debated from 1996 to 2023. It was settled near-unanimously by the Constitution (106th Amendment) Act, 2023. Both the ruling party and the full opposition voted for it. This debate is constitutionally closed.

Debate B: Delimitation & Seat Expansion ✗ Ongoing

Should India expand Lok Sabha from 543 to 850 seats? Should seat allocation shift to population-proportional basis — reducing southern states' share? Who controls the timing of delimitation? This is a separate, older constitutional question that predates women's reservation by decades.

How they got linked: The 2023 Act conditioned women's reservation on a post-Census delimitation (Article 334A). This created a chain reaction — any delimitation now inevitably reopens the seat-expansion and north-south representation debate. The April 2026 bills tried to resolve both at once, bundling women's reservation with a broad constitutional restructuring. Opposition parties who voted for the 2023 Act voted against the 2026 bills. The government argued the bills were necessary to operationalise women's reservation before 2029. Opposition parties argued the bills bundled women's reservation with a seat-expansion and reallocation exercise they considered a separate — and contested — constitutional question..

❌

April 17, 2026: The 131st Amendment Bill is Defeated — A Historic First

298 votes in favour, 230 against. Of 528 members present, the government needed 352 (two-thirds majority under Article 368) and fell short by 54 votes. The government withdrew the Delimitation Bill and UT Laws Bill immediately after. This was the first time a constitutional amendment introduced by the Modi government failed in the Lok Sabha. The Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam (2023) remains law and was officially notified as in force on April 16, 2026 — but women's reservation still cannot activate without a completed delimitation exercise.

The Women's Reservation Debate — Settled by 2023 Act
What Is Already in the Constitution
  • 33% of Lok Sabha seats reserved for women — enacted (Art. 330A)
  • 33% of every state assembly reserved — enacted (Art. 332A)
  • SC/ST sub-quota within women's seats — enacted
  • 15-year sunset clause, extendable by Parliament — enacted (Art. 334A)
  • Seats rotate after each delimitation — enacted
  • All major parties voted in favour in September 2023
What Remains Unresolved in This Debate
  • OBC sub-quota — not enacted; still a live demand from SP, RJD and allies
  • Rajya Sabha and state Legislative Council reservation — not addressed
  • Implementation date — still contingent on a delimitation exercise that has not happened
The Delimitation Debate — Separate, Ongoing
The North-South Fault Line

India's Lok Sabha seats were frozen based on 1971 Census data — to prevent states with effective family planning from losing seats. Population-proportional delimitation using 2011 data would mean:

  • Tamil Nadu: 39 → ~32 seats (−7)
  • Kerala: 20 → ~15 seats (−5)
  • Uttar Pradesh: 80 → ~89 seats (+9)
  • Bihar: 40 → ~46 seats (+6)
Why Opposition Voted Against in 2026
  • Southern state parties feared a permanently diminished political voice
  • The constitutional amendment gave Parliament (simple majority) power over timing of delimitation — weakening Rajya Sabha's oversight role
  • The bill would have changed the Lok Sabha-to-Rajya Sabha seat ratio from 2.2:1 to 3.3:1 — tilting power in joint sittings toward the government
  • Opposition argued these were unrelated to women's reservation and should not be bundled with it
The Three Bills — Introduced April 16, Voted Down / Withdrawn April 17, 2026
❌ Constitution (131st Amendment) Bill — DEFEATED (298–230)
The Core Amendment

Would have expanded Lok Sabha maximum from 550 to 850 seats (815 from states + 35 from UTs). Reverted to population-proportional seat allocation. Gave Parliament authority via simple majority to choose which Census to use. Amended Article 334A to delink women's reservation from Census 2027. Required 352 votes; received 298.

↩ Delimitation Bill, 2026 — WITHDRAWN
The Mechanism

Would have constituted a Delimitation Commission using 2011 Census data (the latest published census, as 2021 was postponed and 2027 Census not yet completed). Withdrawn after the 131st Amendment failed — could not proceed without the constitutional foundation.

↩ UT Laws (Amendment) Bill — WITHDRAWN
The Extension

Would have applied parallel seat-expansion and delimitation provisions to Puducherry, Delhi, and Jammu & Kashmir assemblies. Also withdrawn after the constitutional amendment fell. Women's reservation for Delhi and Puducherry remains legally intact under the 2023 Act — but similarly dormant.

Where Does Women's Reservation Go From Here?

Current Legal Status (as of April 2026)
  • The 106th Amendment (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam) was officially notified as in force on April 16, 2026
  • Article 334A still conditions activation on post-Census delimitation
  • Census 2027 reference date: March 1, 2027
  • Last delimitation exercise (2002) took 6 years to complete
  • Women's reservation cannot realistically apply to 2029 elections
  • Earliest plausible implementation: 2034 Lok Sabha elections
Possible Paths Forward
  • A narrower bill that only removes the Census-delimitation condition from Article 334A — without changing seat numbers or allocation formulas — might win broader support
  • Government could bring revised bills in a future session decoupling seat expansion from women's reservation
  • If no legislative fix: reservation activates via Census 2027 + delimitation, but not before 2034
  • The political pressure will intensify as 2029 elections approach with reservation still inoperative

The 2023 Act was passed unanimously. The 2026 bills failed 298 to 230. The difference was not a change of heart on women's reservation — it was the delimitation and seat-expansion package that was bundled with it. The clearest path forward may be what the opposition actually asked for: delink the two issues and legislate them separately.

— Synthesis from analyses of the Constitution (131st Amendment) Bill, 2026 and the Constitution (106th Amendment) Act, 2023
